This page is a resource guide to learn core terms that come up in conversations about technology and power: implicit bias, the Implicit Association Test (IAT), and intersectionality.

1. Understanding Implicit Bias

Implicit bias refers to attitudes or stereotypes that can affect our understanding, actions, and decisions in an unconscious way. Even when we believe we’re being fair, our brains can still rely on quick mental shortcuts shaped by culture, history, and media.

Key Resource: APA: Implicit Bias Overview

A beginner-friendly explanation of what implicit bias is and why it matters.

2. The Implicit Association Test (IAT)

The Implicit Association Test (IAT) measures the strength of associations between concepts (like race or gender) and evaluations (like “good” or “bad”). It uses reaction time to capture automatic patterns — not what you *want* to believe, but what your brain may do quickly without you noticing.

Try it yourself: Project Implicit (Harvard)

The official site where you can take the IAT in several categories.

3. Intersectionality

Intersectionality is a framework for understanding how multiple identities overlap (like race, gender, class, sexuality, disability) and shape a person’s lived experiences. In other words, people don’t experience the world through only one identity at a time — those identities interact and can change how power and discrimination show up.

Course Video: What is Intersectionality? (PragerU)

A short video overview that represents one perspective in the public debate about intersectionality.

Deeper Background: Kimberlé Crenshaw (selected writing)

Foundational work connected to the concept (more academic/advanced).

4. Course Readings: Connecting Bias to Technology and Power

In LIS 500, we connect bias to technology by asking who is represented, who is excluded, and how systems can reproduce inequality. These course readings help build that lens.

Assigned Reading: Lisa Nakamura — “Indigenous Circuits”

Explores identity, representation, and power in digital spaces.

Assigned Reading: Robin DiAngelo — “White Fragility”

Discusses defensiveness and resistance that can appear when race and power are addressed.

5. More Resources to Keep Learning

If you want to go beyond the basics, these resources connect implicit bias and intersectionality to real-world technology issues like AI, facial recognition, and data ethics.

  • TED Talk — Verna Myers: “How to overcome our biases” (approachable and practical)
  • Data & Society Research Institute (tech + society research)
  • Brookings — How algorithms can perpetuate bias (how bias shows up in systems)

If you’re new to this topic, start with the TED talk and the APA page, then explore the others.
